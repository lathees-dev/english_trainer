Rules for Using Adjectives

An adjective is a word that describes or modifies a noun or pronoun, adding detail or emphasis. Here are the essential rules for using adjectives:

Types of Adjectives:

  • Descriptive Adjectives: Describe qualities (e.g., happy, blue, tall).
  • Quantitative Adjectives: Indicate quantity or number (e.g., some, many, three).
  • Demonstrative Adjectives: Point to specific nouns (e.g., this, that, these, those).
  • Possessive Adjectives: Show ownership (e.g., my, your, his, her).
  • Interrogative Adjectives: Used in questions (e.g., which, what, whose).
  • Comparative Adjectives: Compare two things (e.g., smarter, bigger).
  • Superlative Adjectives: Compare three or more things (e.g., smartest, biggest).

Placement of Adjectives:

  • Before a Noun: Most adjectives come before the noun (e.g., a tall building).
  • After a Linking Verb: Some adjectives follow linking verbs (e.g., The sky is blue).

Order of Multiple Adjectives:

When using multiple adjectives, the general order is: opinion, size, age, shape, color, proper adjective, purpose (e.g., a beautiful big old round green French wooden table).

Agreement in Number:

Adjectives generally do not change form for singular or plural nouns in English (e.g., big dog and big dogs).

Comparative and Superlative Forms:

  • Add -er for one-syllable adjectives (e.g., taller), or use more for two-syllable adjectives or more (e.g., more beautiful).
  • Add -est for one-syllable adjectives (e.g., tallest), or use most for two or more syllables (e.g., most beautiful).
  • Irregular forms: good → better → best, bad → worse → worst.

Avoid Double Adjectives:

Avoid using two adjectives that mean the same thing (e.g., She is a lovely beautiful girl should be She is a lovely girl).

Adjectives with “-ed” and “-ing”:

  • -ed adjectives: Describe feelings (e.g., bored, excited, tired).
  • -ing adjectives: Describe characteristics of things (e.g., boring, exciting, tiring).

Example: I am bored vs. This is a boring movie.

Adjective + Noun Combinations:

Adjectives can form compound nouns (e.g., snow-white, well-known, high-pitched), often hyphenated.

Demonstrative and Possessive Adjectives:

  • Demonstrative Adjectives: Point to specific nouns (e.g., this, that, these, those).
  • Possessive Adjectives: Indicate possession (e.g., my, your, his, her).

Intensifiers:

Intensifiers modify adjectives to strengthen or weaken their meaning (e.g., very, extremely, quite, somewhat).

Example: She is extremely talented.

Adjective Pairs and Comparison:

Use more and most with two or more syllable adjectives (e.g., more interesting, most expensive), and -er and -est with one-syllable adjectives (e.g., smaller, smallest).
