Rules for Using Conjunctions

A conjunction is a word that connects words, phrases, clauses, or sentences. Conjunctions help to form more complex sentences, showing the relationship between ideas or elements within a sentence.

Types of Conjunctions:

Coordinating Conjunctions:

These conjunctions connect words, phrases, or independent clauses that are of equal importance.

FANBOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So)

  • For: Shows cause or reason.
    Example: She was tired, for she had been working all day.
  • And: Adds similar information.
    Example: I like coffee and tea.
  • Nor: Presents an alternative negative.
    Example: She doesn't like cats, nor does she like dogs.
  • But: Shows contrast.
    Example: He wanted to go, but he was feeling sick.
  • Or: Presents an alternative.
    Example: Would you prefer tea or coffee?
  • Yet: Shows contrast or unexpected result.
    Example: She studied hard, yet she failed the test.
  • So: Shows result or consequence.
    Example: It was raining, so we stayed inside.

Subordinating Conjunctions:

These conjunctions connect an independent clause with a dependent clause, showing a relationship between them.

  • Examples include: although, because, since, if, unless, when, while, as, until, after, before, even though, etc.
  • Example: I stayed home because it was raining.
  • Example: He will call you when he gets home.

Correlative Conjunctions:

These are pairs of conjunctions used to link equivalent elements in a sentence.

  • Examples include: either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also, whether...or.
  • Example: Either you come with me, or I’ll go alone.
  • Example: Not only did she win the race, but also she broke the record.

Conjunctive Adverbs:

These words function as both conjunctions and adverbs, connecting independent clauses with a semicolon or period and sometimes introducing a transitional element.

  • Examples include: however, therefore, moreover, thus, consequently, otherwise, nonetheless.
  • Example: I wanted to go to the park; however, it started to rain.
  • Example: She didn’t study for the test; therefore, she didn’t pass.

Rules for Using Conjunctions:

Coordinate Clauses with Coordinating Conjunctions:

Use coordinating conjunctions to link equal elements (e.g., two independent clauses, two words, or two phrases).

  • Example: I went to the store, and I bought some fruits.
  • Example: She likes both apples and oranges.

Subordinate Clauses with Subordinating Conjunctions:

Use subordinating conjunctions to connect a dependent clause with an independent clause, showing time, cause, condition, contrast, etc.

  • Example: I will go to the park if it stops raining.
  • Example: Although it was cold, we went for a walk.

Use Correlative Conjunctions Together:

Always use both parts of correlative conjunctions together (e.g., either...or, neither...nor).

  • Example: Both John and Mary attended the meeting.
  • Example: She is neither rich nor famous.

Use Conjunctive Adverbs to Link Sentences:

When using conjunctive adverbs, place them after a semicolon and use a comma after the adverb.

  • Example: I wanted to go for a walk; however, it started to rain.
  • Example: She was tired; therefore, she went to bed early.

Avoid Run-On Sentences:

When joining two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction, ensure a comma is used before the conjunction if the clauses are long or complex.

  • Example: I love to read books, but I don't have much time these days.

Punctuation with Correlative Conjunctions:

Correlative conjunctions do not require additional punctuation between the elements they connect unless needed for clarity.

  • Example: Either you call him, or I will.

Avoid Double Conjunctions:

Do not use two conjunctions unnecessarily in the same sentence.

  • Incorrect: But and I like ice cream.
  • Correct: But I like ice cream.
